ABOUT

What Makes Muso-kai Karate Different

I

Karate with Books

Our training is grounded in founder Kiyoshi Arakaki's book series Okinawa Budō Karate no Gokui (The Essence of Okinawan Budo Karate). Rather than relying on intuition and oral tradition alone, you learn a technical system explained through anatomy and physics — through books and training together.

II

Technique, Not Muscle

Traditional Okinawan karate generates energy from the drop of gravity and the use of the hips. Because progress does not depend on age or build, many of our members begin in their 40s and 50s.

III

A Budo Without Tournaments

There are no competitive matches. Instead of trading points, we work step by step to absorb the original combat principles preserved in kata such as Naifanchi and Pinan.

The Meaning of Our Name and Symbol

The name "Muso-kai" comes from munen-musō, a state of mind free of all distracting thoughts. It is also drawn from the words of Master Arakaki — "Those who contemplate Nothingness bring forth Something for the world."

Our symbol places the seiken — the fore-fist of karate — inside the mitsudomoe, the crest of the Shō royal family of the Ryukyu Kingdom and the emblem of the kingdom's flag. The royal crest, called the Hidari Gomon, swirls to the left; Muso-kai's symbol turns to the right out of respect for the Shō family and the Ryukyu Kingdom. It carries a wish: that karate, born in Okinawa and now spread across the world, will contribute to the peace and prosperity of the world — and that this role will, in time, return as a contribution to Okinawa itself.

FOUNDER

About the Founder

Kiyoshi ArakakiPresident, International Okinawa Karate-do Muso-kai

Born in 1954 in Naha, Okinawa — the birthplace of karate — as the eldest son of a medical family that ran a surgical hospital. With Shinei Kyan, a 9th-dan master of Matsubayashi-ryu, among his mother's relatives, he grew up close to karate from early childhood. He went on to research and systematize the body mechanics of traditional Okinawan karate — lost as sport karate spread around the world — from the standpoint of anatomy and physics, and founded the International Okinawa Karate-do Muso-kai.

His books, the Okinawa Budō Karate no Gokui series, made a major impact on the karate world, and he holds seminars in Japan and overseas. He continues to teach in person at study groups across Japan, including Okinawa.

HISTORY

The Story of Muso-kai

How one karateka arrived at the lost principles of the old art — the journey of founder Kiyoshi Arakaki.

1954

Born in Naha, Okinawa

Born in Naha, the eldest son of a doctor of medicine who ran a surgical hospital. Among his mother's relatives was Shinei Kyan, a 9th-dan master of Matsubayashi-ryu who also served in Japan's House of Councillors, and he grew up with a longing for karate from an early age.

Youth

Training in Shuri-te

In junior high school he helped found a karate club — still a rarity at the time — and received thorough instruction in the kata of Shuri-te, built on Naifanchi, from Tenei Taba, an early student of Master Shoshin Nagamine. During his years at Naha High School, he entered Master Nagamine's dojo. In an era when "one person, one art" traditions still survived across Okinawa, he sought out many of the so-called hidden bushi.

1980

Founding Muso-kai in Utah, USA

After moving to the United States and coaching a university karate club, he opened the Muso-kai dojo in Utah in 1980.

1980s

Success in Competition

He and his students placed highly in international full-contact and kōshiki (protective-gear) karate tournaments, and he came to host Utah's largest national tournament twice a year. In 1987 he was appointed president of the U.S. Koshiki Karate Federation and awarded 6th dan (he now holds 7th dan). The influence of Joko Ninomiya, founder of Enshin Kaikan, also dates from this period.

Turning
point

Calcutta, India

As a volunteer at Mother Teresa's Home for the Dying in Calcutta, he cared for many people in their final hours. Born into a family devoted to saving lives, yet devoted himself to an art whose ultimate end is to defeat another — facing that contradiction, he arrived at the principle: "Those who contemplate Nothingness bring forth Something for the world."

5 years of
retraining

Starting Over from the Beginning

"I could perform the kata, but I had not understood the ti — the art itself as a martial art." Confronting this fact, he halted all of his flourishing tournament and teaching activities and retrained from scratch in the old kata, beginning with Naifanchi. It was a genuinely difficult period, marked by financial hardship.

5 more
years

Rebuilding the Teaching System

Okinawan karate had been handed down one-to-one, from master to student. He spent roughly five more years establishing a way to teach it to many students at once. The result is Muso-kai's present system, in which everything from basics to kumite is unified by the principles of Naifanchi.

To the
world

Publication of Okinawa Budō Karate no Gokui

The body mechanics he regained through retraining were published as the Okinawa Budō Karate no Gokui series, which came to be known as the "Arakaki Theory" and has been translated into English, Spanish, and Italian. In Okinawa Karate-dō no Rekishi (The History of Okinawan Karate-do, 2011, Hara Shobo) and "Heian no Kata" no Kenshō (A Study of the Pinan Kata, 2013, Hara Shobo), he shed light on the karate of the Ryukyu Kingdom era and on the Pinan kata. Today he holds seminars around the world, and study groups have been founded across Japan.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need any athletic experience?
No. Muso-kai training centers on learning body mechanics that do not rely on strength or explosive power, so people with no athletic background — or little confidence in their fitness — can start comfortably.
Is there an age limit?
We have an adult program only, open from junior-high age and up (there are no children's classes). Many members begin in their 40s or 50s, and there is no upper age limit.
I train in another style. Can I join?
You are very welcome. Practitioners of sport karate and other martial arts are often the most surprised by the answers to the question, "Why does the kata move this way?" You may also keep training in another style alongside Muso-kai.
Can I just observe?
Yes, observation is available (first visit only, ¥1,000). Please choose the observation option in the application form.
What should I bring to my first class?
Just comfortable clothes (your gi, if you have one) and a drink.
Are there tournaments?
Muso-kai holds no competitive matches. Through kata and body mechanics, we practice karate as a budo you can continue for life.
